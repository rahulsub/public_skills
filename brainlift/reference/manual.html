Field Manual · Knowledge Craft

The BrainLift

A structured document engineered to manufacture a defensible point of view — not to store what you know, but to change what you're able to think.

What it is

  • A layered climb from fact to opinion, on one narrow topic
  • A curation engine — sources chosen and rejected on purpose
  • The substrate a person or an agent reasons from

What it isn't

  • A notes dump, a link list, or a summary of "the field"
  • A wiki that tries to be complete and neutral
  • Finished — it holds live disputes rather than resolving them
01 · The core idea

Generic knowledge produces generic output.

Everything a BrainLift does serves one end: encoding a specific worldview densely enough that anything built on top of it — an essay, a product call, an agent's behavior — inherits a real point of view instead of the averaged-out consensus. The lower layers exist to earn the right to hold the top one.

02 · Anatomy

Four strata of depth (DOK)

The spine of every BrainLift is an ascending stack of Depth-of-Knowledge layers. Each is built by working on the layer beneath it. Position is meaning: bedrock at the base, the spike at the summit.

4Summit

Spiky Points of View

// the payload — opinionated & divergent

Defensible stances that disagree with the field, with the reasoning that earns them. This is what the whole structure exists to produce. If it wouldn't make an expert argue, it isn't DOK4.

Built from DOK3Test: Would a smart peer push back?
3Ascent

Non-Obvious Insights

// what falls out of comparing summaries

Cross-cutting observations that only appear when you hold several DOK2 summaries against each other. The things a careful practitioner notices that never make it into the intro material.

Built from DOK2Test: Is it absent from the textbook?
2Strata

Compressed Summaries

// facts synthesized into claims

Consensus, compressed. What a competent person in the field would sign off on without argument. Not opinion yet — the honest common ground the insights will later cut against.

Built from DOK1Test: Would the field agree?
1Bedrock

Foundational Facts

// atomic & verifiable

The ground truth: single, checkable statements with a source. No synthesis, no spin. If a claim can't be pinned to something verifiable, it doesn't belong on the bedrock.

03 · The build sequence

How to actually make one

The order matters — this is a genuine sequence, not a menu. Skipping a rung is the most common way a BrainLift comes out hollow.

01

Pin the scope

One topic, sharply bounded. "Shell startup performance," not "developer tooling." A BrainLift about everything is a failed BrainLift.

02

Curate sources — including who you reject

Name the experts you deliberately follow and the ones you deliberately don't. The rejections carry as much signal as the picks.

03

Lay the bedrock (DOK1)

Extract atomic, sourced facts. Resist the urge to editorialize — you're pouring foundation, not framing yet.

04

Compress upward (DOK2 → DOK3)

Summarize the facts, then hold the summaries against each other until the non-obvious insights surface. This friction is where DOK3 is born.

05

Commit the spike (DOK4)

State the opinions you'll defend, with the ladder beneath each one visible. Where two spikes genuinely conflict, keep both — flag it disputed, don't resolve it prematurely.

06

Enforce the caps

Trim each layer back to its hard limit. The compression is the product — see §06.

04 · Good vs bad, layer by layer

The same topic, lifted and sunk

One subject carried up all four rungs — done well, then done badly. The failures aren't wrong facts; they're claims sitting on the wrong layer.

Worked subject:zsh startup time// on a fast Apple-silicon machine
1 Bedrock
✓ Lifts

"Zinit's wait turbo mode defers plugin sourcing until after the first prompt paints. A cold zsh -i -c exit here measures 210 ms baseline."

Atomic, sourced, falsifiable — you could re-run it.

✕ Sinks

"Zsh can feel kind of slow to start sometimes."

Not atomic, not measured, nothing to verify. That's a vibe, not a fact.

2 Summary
✓ Lifts

"Most startup cost is synchronous plugin sourcing; deferring it is the single highest-leverage lever available."

Compresses several facts into a claim the field would accept.

✕ Sinks

"Turbo mode defers plugins until after the first prompt."

Just DOK1 restated. A summary must synthesize, not echo.

3 Insight
✓ Lifts

"The headline percentage is measured against a bloated baseline. On an already-lean config the absolute saving collapses to ~10 ms — below the threshold of perception."

Only visible by comparing summaries; contradicts the marketing framing.

✕ Sinks

"Turbo mode makes zsh faster."

Obvious, and true at DOK2 already. An insight has to cost something to reach.

4 Spike
✓ Lifts

"Chasing sub-100 ms shell startup is procrastination dressed as craft. Past ~150 ms the only honest optimization left is measuring less often."

Opinionated, divergent, defensible — a peer would want to argue.

✕ Sinks

"A fast shell is good and worth setting up well."

Consensus mush. Nobody disagrees, so it moves no one.

◈ Hold the dispute — don't resolve it
Claim A · importance 0.72

"Turbo mode cuts zsh startup by roughly 40% on this machine."

Claim B · importance 0.72

"Turbo mode saves closer to 10 ms in absolute terms — the percentage flatters a bloated baseline."

Both are DOK4, both defensible, and they genuinely conflict. A mature BrainLift keeps the pair status: disputed and visible. Flattening it to one "answer" would destroy the exact tension that makes the document worth reading.

05 · Failure modes

What makes a BrainLift bad

Almost every weak BrainLift fails in one of these ways. Learn to name them and you can fix them on sight.

  • ✕
    The pancake. All DOK1 and DOK2, no ascent. A tidy summary of the field with zero opinion — informative and completely inert.
  • ✕
    The soapbox. Straight to DOK4 with no ladder beneath it. Opinions no reader can trace back to a fact — indistinguishable from a rant.
  • ✕
    Layer inversion. Opinion filed as fact, or obvious restatement filed as insight. The content might be fine; it's sitting on the wrong stratum.
  • ✕
    Consensus mush. A "spiky" POV nobody would argue with. If it can't provoke a knowledgeable peer, it isn't DOK4 — it's a DOK2 in a costume.
  • ✕
    The sprawl. Scope creep and soft caps. It keeps absorbing adjacent topics until the compression is gone and it's just a wiki again.
  • ✕
    Premature resolution. Two real, conflicting DOK4 views quietly merged into one bland "answer." The dispute was the value; averaging it away is a product-defining failure.
  • ✕
    Uncurated intake. Every source treated as equal, none rejected. Curation with no rejections isn't curation — it's a bookmark folder.
06 · The non-negotiable

Hard caps, not soft caps.

The single most consequential setting in a BrainLift is the limit on each layer's output. Those caps are hard. A BrainLift that lets its DOK layers sprawl "just this once" collapses back into a knowledge dump — and the compression is the product. The whole value proposition is that a reader gets the distilled worldview, not the raw material it was rendered from.

Treat any soft-cap setting as a product-defining failure, not a convenience. The moment you relax the caps to fit more in, you've stopped building a BrainLift and started building the thing it was supposed to replace.

07 · One-page spec

Ship-ready checklist

Run a finished BrainLift against this before you trust anything built on top of it.

Structure

  • Scope is one narrow, named topic
  • All four DOK layers present
  • Each layer built from the one below
  • Nothing sits on the wrong stratum

The spike

  • At least one POV a peer would contest
  • Every opinion traces to a fact
  • Conflicts kept as disputed pairs
  • No consensus mush at DOK4

Sources

  • Curated, not collected
  • Rejections named, not just picks
  • DOK1 claims each carry a source

Discipline

  • Every layer within its hard cap
  • No scope creep since last pass
  • Reads as a POV, not a wiki
